The third week of July delivers dense signals across project completions, urban renewal, and technological breakthroughs. Kuma Tower: How a Supertall Named After Its Architect Responds to Taichung's Urban Character
Cross-verified: CTEE · Mirror Media · UDN · My Housing

On July 16, Kengo Kuma appeared in Taichung's Seventh Redevelopment Zone to attend the topping-out ceremony of "Kuma Tower" (Lien-Ju Chung-Wei). At 42 floors above ground, seven below, and 208 meters in height, it is not only Kuma's tallest building to date but also the only skyscraper in the world to carry his surname—an act of architectural authorship unusual in the anonymized world of commercial high-rises.

Design concept: symbiosis of people, building, and nature. Kuma stated he participated from site selection onward, with the core design philosophy being coexistence between humans and the environment—a response to Taichung's famously green urban character. The 2,380-ping site preserves approximately 1,500 ping of open green space, conceived as an urban park where office workers and citizens can share the landscape.

Façade strategy: timber-textured baffles as climate device. The building's most distinctive visual feature—layered timber-textured aluminum baffles—is not decorative. These baffles modulate daylight penetration, reduce solar heat gain, and create continuously shifting shadow patterns throughout the day. The façade contractor revealed that over 100,000 construction drawings were produced for the curtain wall system alone. Since each baffle differs in dimension and angle, every penetration and flexible joint requires individual verification, with control joint tolerances set at 12 millimeters.

Spatial organization: 360-degree glazing and livable balconies. Office floor plates of 80–150 ping (with full floors reaching 760 ping) offer flexible configurations. The 360-degree glazing maximizes natural light, while livable balconies on every floor challenge the sealed-envelope paradigm of conventional office towers. A four-story retail podium at the base will host international dining and café brands.

Structural safety and sustainability. The building is pursuing three certifications: seismic design, seismic construction, and green building. Located in Taiwan's western seismic zone, the structural demands of a 208-meter tower present an intriguing formal tension with Kuma's signature language of lightness, transparency, and material tactility. Whether a building this large can remain faithful to that language remains an open question—one the completed tower will answer.

Grosvenor Square Reborn: How an 18th-Century London Square Became a 21st-Century Climate-Adaptive Public Space
Cross-verified: BBC News · Grosvenor Property UK

On July 20, Grosvenor Square—one of London's oldest and most historically significant public squares—reopened after a year of construction and a decade of planning. Lead landscape architect Chris Stanton called it an "extraordinary transformation," and the term is justified: what was once largely manicured lawn and tarmac paths is now a layered, biodiverse ecosystem designed for a warming city.

From lawn to biodiversity infrastructure. Over 150,000 new plants—specifically selected to withstand rising urban temperatures—have replaced the uniform grass, alongside 40 new trees, two wetland areas, and 300 additional seats. The design draws on the square's original 18th-century layout while embedding contemporary environmental science: the historical axes remain legible, but the planting palette and drainage strategy are calibrated to future climate projections rather than past traditions. This marks a paradigm shift from "preservation" to "adaptation" in historic landscape management.

Climate resilience as a design parameter. Grosvenor Property UK CEO Jamie Whitty-Lewis was explicit about the climate rationale: "We are feeling the summers getting warmer and the winters getting wetter. The planting here has a cooling effect—you'll feel right now it's actually quite cool here under the trees." The project represents a growing recognition that heritage landscapes must be retrofitted not only for amenity but for urban microclimate regulation.

The re-publicization of urban space. The Duke of Westminster described the square as "a place where people come and stop and are able to relax and sit, find their own space." This language—of public dwelling rather than private possession—signals a broader shift in how elite urban estates are repositioning themselves in the climate era. Parallel initiatives are unfolding globally: Venice citizens' group Poveglia per Tutti recently won a six-year concession to transform an abandoned lagoon island into public green space; Madrid's Parque Castellana will reclaim 70,000 m² from traffic with a 35-meter solar canopy; Mumbai's Kala Ghoda district has just launched its first pedestrian-only cultural quarter. From London to Mumbai, cities are weaving climate adaptation and public space accessibility into a single design agenda.

A Double Milestone for 3D-Printed Construction: Europe's Largest Apartment Building + Ireland's First Cement-Free Geopolymer
Cross-verified: COBOD · 3D Printing Industry · Trinity College Dublin · New Atlas

This week, 3D-printed construction reached two distinct but equally important milestones: one a productivity validation from an active construction site, the other a materials breakthrough from a laboratory.

ViliaSprint²: full-scale productivity verified. In Bezannes, France, ViliaSprint²—Europe's largest 3D-printed multi-family building (800 m², three stories, 12 social housing units)—was developed by Plurial Novilia and printed by PERI 3D Construction using a COBOD BOD2 gantry system. The shell was printed in 34 active days (versus 50 planned), reducing overall project timeline by three months compared to a near-identical conventional building constructed simultaneously on the same site as a control group. Results: shell construction time halved, operators reduced from six to three, waste cut from 10% to 5%, and an additional ~10% concrete savings achieved through optimized curved geometries enabled by 3D printing.

Holcim supplied the printable concrete based on its TectorPrint technology, reinforced with synthetic macro fibers and formulated within the low-carbon ECOPact range. The building integrates 500 m² of photovoltaic panels and achieves approximately 60% energy self-sufficiency. Plurial Novilia is planning a follow-on project of approximately 40 apartments deploying two printers simultaneously.

Trinity College Dublin: a materials breakthrough. Researchers at Trinity's School of Engineering demonstrated Ireland's first cement-free, 3D-printed geopolymer at Harcourt Technologies Ltd. The distinctive red-brown material uses bauxite refining residue—containing no Portland cement, with over 30% of its cement-like content from local industrial waste. Preliminary assessment suggests approximately 70% lower embodied carbon compared to conventional Portland cement concrete.

Professor Sara Pavia described the material as "a flexible binder platform whose composition, rheology, setting behavior, and early-age performance can be tailored to different manufacturing processes and construction applications." The next challenge is scaling from laboratory batches to industrial quantities, focusing on mechanical performance, durability, reinforcement compatibility, long-term stability, and regulatory compliance.

Viewing the two stories together reveals two parallel development axes: a process axis (productivity, speed, cost-competitiveness), represented by ViliaSprint², aiming to move 3D printing from pilot to mainstream; and a materials axis (low-carbon, circular, locally sourced), represented by Trinity's geopolymer, aiming to fundamentally restructure the carbon equation of construction. The fusion of both axes—high-speed printing with ultra-low-carbon materials—has not yet arrived, but is rapidly approaching.

MIT Develops Topology Optimization Framework That Incorporates Real Printer Constraints for 3D-Printed Bridges
Source: MIT News
MIT researchers published a new framework in Additive Manufacturing that embeds a 3D concrete printer's real fabrication limits (nozzle thickness, turning radius, continuous printing) directly into topology optimization, producing designs buildable without manual redesign. A 2.3-meter concrete bridge validated the approach.

Source: ESG Vision
NCKU's C-Cube Lab leads a seven-team Fab-X consortium developing a low-carbon building system using locally sourced Kaohsiung soil as 3D printing feedstock and alkali-activated binders (blast furnace slag, fly ash, reservoir silt) to replace cement. The system achieves at least 25% carbon reduction, with costs at 1.1× conventional materials.

Arup and YJK have launched AI Designer in Hong Kong, a digital platform optimizing structural design and pile foundation layout. Germany's DREIHAUS Project Pilots First Domestic Application of evoZero Carbon-Captured Cement

Produced in Brevik, Norway, the facility captures 400,000 tonnes of CO₂ annually. Two of three buildings use recyclable evoBuild, the third uses evoZero.

Venice Architecture Biennale 2027 Takes Shape: National Pavilions Illuminate Wang Shu and Lu Wenyu's "Coexistence" Proposition
Cross-verified: ArchDaily · La Biennale · The Architect's Newspaper · Epoch Times · China Ministry of Culture

With less than ten months until the 20th International Architecture Exhibition—La Biennale di Venezia opens on May 8, 2027, national pavilion curatorial proposals are being unveiled at an accelerating pace. Curated by Wang Shu and Lu Wenyu under the theme "Do Architecture—For the Possibility of Coexistence Facing a Real Reality," the Biennale positions coexistence as architecture's ethical core in an era of climate breakdown, geopolitical tension, and resource inequality.

Austria: conceding the pavilion to a nation without one. The most conceptually ambitious proposal comes from Austria. "Koncesija / Konzession / Concession(e)," curated by architects Adna and Ajna Babahmetović with Sebastian Höglinger, proposes temporarily granting Austria's Giardini pavilion to Bosnia and Herzegovina—which has no national pavilion in the Biennale's historic grounds. The central spatial installation reconstructs the lobby of Sarajevo's Holiday Inn Hotel (designed by Ivan Štraus for the 1984 Winter Olympics) inside Josef Hoffmann's 1934 pavilion. The proposal problematizes the very structure of national representation: when a country's architecture cannot be exhibited by its own nation, can "architectural diplomacy" serve as an alternative?

Taiwan: institutional transition. Taiwan's pavilion has undergone a significant institutional shift. Starting in 2026, management transferred from the National Taiwan Museum of Fine Arts to the Taiwan Museum Foundation—a move seen as groundwork for a future Taiwan Architecture Museum. The selected team, Yushan Workshop, was praised by the jury for responding to "the resilience, hybridity, and innovation under constraint that characterize Taiwanese architectural culture."

United States: deadline July 23, grant increased to $475,000. The U.S. Department of State's call for pavilion proposals closes in three days, seeking "innovative and compelling architectural designs that exemplify America's exceptionalism." China's pavilion call closes September 30. Concurrently, the UIA World Congress in Barcelona features China's Pavilion presenting 350+ architectural practices under "A Dynamic View of Contemporary Chinese Architecture."

Viewed together, the emerging proposals suggest a latent narrative: Wang and Lu's "coexistence" is not functioning as a top-down theme to be illustrated but as an open question that each pavilion redefines in its own terms—Austria through spatial concession, Taiwan through island resilience, the U.S. through the unresolved tension between "exceptionalism" and "coexistence."
