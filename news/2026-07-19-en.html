KPF's TP-Link Headquarters in Shenzhen: From "Fortress" to "Public Infrastructure"—A Paradigm Shift in Tech Campus Typology

Cross-verified: Dezeen · e-architect · Shenzhen · 2026

The corporate headquarters has long been among architecture's most closed typologies—walls, security checkpoints, inward-facing campus layouts. Within the span of weeks, two major Shenzhen projects have challenged this orthodoxy: MAD's Tengyun Center for Tencent lifts its main mass 8.6 meters into the air, returning the ground plane to the public; and KPF's TP-Link LXD campus in Qianhai weaves sports courts, greenery, and social spaces into the building envelope through a one-kilometer "Landscape Ribbon."

Design concept. KPF's scheme is driven by the imperative to create "shared, open green spaces on a compact site." Rather than relegating public amenities to podium-level pocket parks or standalone landscaping, the design connects two office towers and a residential tower with a peripheral stepped ribbon that varies in width—expanding into basketball courts and running tracks in some sections, contracting to planted terraces and social steps in others. The term "ribbon" itself captures the design's dual nature: it is simultaneously a landscape element (extending the logic of Earthscape Architecture) and a circulation system, consolidating vertical movement, outdoor meetings, and leisure into a single continuous gesture.

Structural strategy. The ribbon's realization depended on close collaboration between KPF and Arup. Each tower's concrete core is offset, with the ribbon's cantilevered sections structurally supported by these offset cores while also providing an alternative means of vertical circulation. Facades combine full-height glazing with perforated metal panels, balancing transparency with solar control.

Context and significance. At 124,600 m², TP-Link LXD accommodates offices, R&D, and housing. The project forms an instructive pair with MAD's Tengyun Center: where MAD opens the ground plane entirely as a coastal public realm, KPF creates a graduated transition between public and private through a continuous landscape band wrapped around the building perimeter. Together, they signal a broader shift in Shenzhen's tech campuses—from corporate islands to nodes in the city's public-space network—echoing global precedents from BIG's Google headquarters to SOM's Huawei campus in Dongguan.

Grosvenor Square Reimagined: London's Most Ambitious Public-Space Climate Adaptation in 25 Years

Cross-verified: BBC News · Grosvenor Property UK · London · Jul 18, 2026

Grosvenor Square, one of central London's oldest and largest public squares, was first laid out in the 18th century by Sir Richard Grosvenor for the people of London. For over a century, it existed primarily as manicured lawns crossed by tarmac paths—an iconic but ecologically impoverished Mayfair landmark. On Monday July 21, following a decade of planning and a year of construction, the square reopens as London's most ambitious public-space climate adaptation project in a quarter-century.

Design strategy. Led by Grosvenor Property UK with landscape architect Chris Stanton, the transformation replaces "grass and tarmac" with a functioning ecosystem: over 150,000 plants selected for heat resilience and urban conditions; 40 new trees augmenting the existing canopy to create layered shade; two new wetland areas for stormwater management and microclimate regulation; and 300 additional seats, dramatically increasing the square's stayability. The project responds directly to observed climate shifts—"summers getting warmer and winters getting wetter," as CEO Jamie Whitty-Lewis noted.

Historical dialogue. While the layout is grounded in contemporary environmental science, spatial elements reference the square's original 18th-century design—not as literal reproduction, but as a revival of the space's original ecological and social intentions through contemporary means. This "historically informed climate adaptation" strategy is emerging as a significant methodology for European historic public spaces, paralleling approaches seen in the Champs-Élysées redesign competition and New York's Madison Square Park microclimate interventions.

Urban significance. The Duke of Westminster, a direct descendant of the square's creator, positioned the reopening as offering "an opportunity for people just to relax a bit and find their own little area." The project marks a clear shift in London's public-space paradigm—from the "great lawn + hardscape" aesthetic of the 2000s toward ecological resilience as the organizing principle. With 300 extra seats, wetland areas, and a café kiosk, the square is designed not for passage but for pause—a calibrated balance of biodiversity, climate function, and public life in the dense urban core.

When Hardware, Not Material, Becomes the Bottleneck: MIT's 3D-Printed Concrete Optimization Framework

Cross-verified: MIT News · Interesting Engineering · Additive Manufacturing (journal) · Jul 15, 2026

Concrete is the world's most-used building material, and its production accounts for roughly 8% of global CO₂ emissions. 3D concrete printing (3DCP) promises a greener alternative: it eliminates labor-intensive formwork and places material only where structurally needed. But the mathematically optimal designs produced by topology optimization—those intricate, spider-web geometries—are often unbuildable by today's large-scale printers. MIT's new framework, published in Additive Manufacturing, closes this gap by embedding a printer's real fabrication limits directly into the optimization itself.

Technical breakthrough. Traditional methods optimize shape first, then require days of manual post-processing to make designs printable. MIT's team adopted mixed-integer optimization, translating three key constraints identified through hands-on collaboration with Autodesk's Boston Technology Center operators—bead width, turning radius, and continuous-path requirement—directly into the mathematical rules. The result: fully printable designs generated in approximately two minutes on a laptop. When the team needed to slightly reduce the bridge's size on printing day, they reran the optimization and had an updated design in 5–10 minutes.

Key finding. The team printed a 2.3-meter compression-only concrete bridge (~410 kg) at Autodesk's facility. It held over 900 kg with virtually no measurable deflection—matching simulations closely. But the study's biggest surprise was this: "From zero to 200,000 pounds, your design is entirely driven by these 'can I build it or not' constraints. And then, after 200,000 pounds, you can start to think about the physics." Current printing hardware, not concrete strength, dictates structural efficiency. The team quantified this: reducing bead width from 4 cm to 1 cm could cut material use by up to 76%—a clear roadmap for printer manufacturers.

Limitations and next steps. The bridge dramatically demonstrated its compression-only nature after testing: when a worker lifted one corner to sweep beneath it, it broke—parts of the bridge were placed in tension they were never designed to carry. The team's next phase targets reinforced concrete 3D printing, though "working out how to feed rebar into a printed concrete structure is proving its own challenge." The bead-width finding, however, provides an actionable hardware improvement target with outsized carbon-reduction returns.

"A Structure of Feeling": A New Generation of Chinese Architects Returns to Aedes Berlin, 25 Years After TUMU

Cross-verified: ArchDaily · Aedes Architecture Forum · Berlin · Jul 4–Oct 14, 2026

In 2001, Aedes Architecture Forum's "TUMU" (土木) exhibition introduced a generation of young independent Chinese architects—including Zhang Yonghe, Liu Jiakun, and Wang Shu—to international attention for the first time. Twenty-five years later, "A Structure of Feeling" turns its gaze toward a new generation working under radically changed conditions. Curated by Gao Changjun (Kris) with academic advice from Li Xiangning (Tongji University), the exhibition presents 12 projects by 9 practices alongside works by three artists.

Curatorial framework. The title draws on Raymond Williams's (1921–1988) concept of the "structure of feeling"—a social experience in the process of becoming, not yet conceptually fixed or ideologically solidified. The exhibition does not ask what Chinese architecture ought to be, but rather: under what forces and within what contradictions is it currently taking shape? Three intertwined dimensions structure the investigation: the urban, the economic-geographical, and the epistemological.

Exhibited works. Atelier Alter Architects (Bu Xiaojun & Zhang Yingfan): Dali Transformer Factory Theatrical District, Yunnan (2023). Atelier XÜK (Liu Kenan & Zhang Xu): Wu Guannan Art Museum, Yixing (2024) and Kunshan Wutong Experimental School (2023). genarchitects (Fan Beilei, Xue Zhe, Kong Rui): Dining & Swimming Complex, Shanghai Zhonghua College (2025). line+ studio (Meng Fanhao): Mansion on Lotus Mountain, Changchun (2022) and Lishui Guyanhuaxiang Art Center (2024). DL Atelier (Liu Yang & Xu Dan): LYCEUM School, Chongzuo, Guangxi (2022). Also featuring Yanfei Architects, Studio QI Architects, and BUZZ | Büro Ziyu Zhuang Architects.

Historical significance. The exhibiting architects share a defining condition: they work outside China's state-owned design institutes and beyond the logic of property-driven mass production—while remaining entangled in both. International educational trajectories have broadened their perspectives; friction with the realities of building has shaped their practice. Notably, the shipping crates that transported the exhibits from China to Berlin by rail have been incorporated into the spatial display—a curatorial detail that foregrounds the inseparable relationship between architectural knowledge production and material flows in a globalized context. The exhibition runs through October 14, 2026 (closed August 20–28).
